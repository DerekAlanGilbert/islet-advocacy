Tegoprubart (Anti-CD40L)

Developed by Eledon Pharmaceuticals, tegoprubart is an anti-CD40 Ligand antibody that serves as the core of a new immunosuppression regimen. Unlike tacrolimus (the current standard), it does not cause kidney damage, neurological toxicity, or the other serious side effects that have historically made islet transplant a difficult trade-off for patients.

The Trial

The Phase I/II trial at the University of Chicago Medicine's Transplant Institute, initially funded by Breakthrough T1D (formerly JDRF), has been expanded from 6 to 12 subjects. A second study has also been funded for patients with both Type 1 diabetes and chronic kidney disease. All six initial patients achieved insulin independence after one or two islet transplants.

Why It Matters

Traditional immunosuppression with calcineurin inhibitors like tacrolimus comes with serious risks: kidney damage, neurological toxicity, increased infection rates, and thromboembolic events. Tegoprubart has shown none of these side effects in the trial so far, making islet transplantation a far more viable path to a functional cure.

The ISLET Act

The Increase Support for Life-saving Endocrine Transplantation (ISLET) Act would reclassify human cadaveric islets -- the insulin-producing cells from the pancreas -- from "biologic drugs" to "organs" under the Public Health Service Act. This single change would dramatically improve patient access to islet transplantation.

What It Does

Currently, islets are regulated as pharmaceutical products, subjecting them to the same complex approval process as manufactured drugs. The ISLET Act would classify them as organs -- which is what they are -- aligning the US with Canada, the European Union, Australia, and Japan, where islets are already regulated this way.

Why It Matters

Reclassification would reduce regulatory barriers, enable more transplant centers to offer the procedure, simplify the path to insurance coverage, and foster competition and innovation. For patients, it means faster access to a treatment that is already showing remarkable results.

June 2023 -- ISLET Act first introduced in Congress
November 2025 -- Reintroduced by Sen. Mike Lee (R-UT) with co-sponsor Sen. Ted Budd (R-NC)
Current Status -- Referred to the Senate Committee on Health, Education, Labor, and Pensions (HELP)

The Administrative Path

Breakthrough T1D has noted that the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) may already have the authority to reclassify islets without new legislation. Both legislative and administrative approaches could achieve the same goal -- the important thing is that reclassification happens. Advocates should push for whichever path moves faster.
